About

I am a Postdoctoral Scholar in the Lewin Lab in the Center for Global Health and Disease at Case Western Reserve University.

I received my PhD from UMass Amherst, where I was advised by Dr. Kristen M. DeAngelis. My dissertation research investigated the effects of microbial diversity on soil carbon formation and ecosystem function.

Currently, my research investigates the role of bacterial strain level diversity and the phage community on vaginal microbiome stability, using a combination of metagenomic and culture-based approaches. Broadly, I am interested in understanding how microbial diversity drives larger, ecosystem scale processes across environmental and human microbiomes.

Research Interests

  • Climate change effects on bacterial diversity

    Across ecosystems, biodiversity is an important driver of ecosystem function. However, climate change threatens to alter this well-established relationship between diversity and function. In Shinfuku et al 2024. I investigated how long-term in situ soil warming affected the relationship between bacterial diversity and ecosystem function at the Harvard Forest Long-Term Ecological Research site. We found that the effects of warming are seasonally dependent, where negative effects of warming on bacterial diversity were observed only in the summer and not in the fall.

  • Bacterial strain level diversity in the vaginal microbiome

    The vaginal microbiome has a significant influence on women's reproductive health. Compared to other human body sites, the bacterial species level diversity can be much lower in the vaginal microbiome. However, often women have multiple strains of the same bacterial species within their microbiomes. I am examining how the diversity of strains within the vaginal microbiome influences the stability of the microbiome.

  • Microbial diversity and soil carbon formation

    Soils are the largest terrestrial store of carbon. Increasing temperatures may make this vast pool of carbon susceptible to increased microbial decomposition, resulting in more CO2 efflux from soils. Yet, paradoxically, much of this stable carbon within soils is the product of microbial metabolism. In Domeignoz-Horta, Shinfuku et al. (2021), we explored the role of microbial diversity in the formation of stable soil carbon using a microbial decomposition microcosm system. We found that the microbial community composition is a signficant driver of stable soil carbon, and that the soil carbon produced from more diverse communities tended to be more stable than soil carbon produced from less diverse communities.
